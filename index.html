What Is Intermittent Fasting? A Complete Guide

Intermittent fasting (IF) is an eating pattern that cycles between periods of fasting and eating. Unlike traditional diets that restrict what you eat, intermittent fasting focuses on when you eat. Research from institutions including Harvard Medical School and Johns Hopkins has linked IF to weight loss, improved insulin sensitivity, reduced inflammation, and enhanced brain function.

The 3 Most Popular IF Protocols

16:8 (Leangains Protocol): Fast for 16 hours, eat within an 8-hour window. The most popular approach — easy to maintain by skipping breakfast and eating between noon and 8 PM. Ideal for beginners and those seeking sustainable fat loss.

18:6: A more aggressive version with an 18-hour fast and 6-hour eating window. Accelerates fat burning while preserving muscle mass when paired with adequate protein intake (2g per kg of bodyweight).

20:4 (Warrior Diet): One large meal and a 4-hour eating window. Best suited for experienced fasters with high discipline. Often combined with resistance training for body recomposition.

How FastingMet Calculates Your Calories

FastingMet uses the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, the gold standard for BMR estimation validated in clinical studies:

  • Men: BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) + 5
  • Women: BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) − 161

Your TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) is then calculated by multiplying your BMR by an activity multiplier (1.2 to 1.725). Your daily calorie target adjusts based on your goal: a 500 kcal deficit for fat loss, a 300 kcal surplus for muscle gain, or maintenance at TDEE.

Optimal Macros for Intermittent Fasting

During your eating window, macro distribution matters significantly. FastingMet calculates:

  • Protein: 2g per kg of bodyweight — essential for muscle preservation during fasting, especially in a caloric deficit.
  • Fats: 25% of your calorie target — supports hormone production and satiety during fasting hours.
  • Carbohydrates: The remaining calories — prioritize complex carbs around your training window for energy and recovery.

Benefits Backed by Science

Multiple peer-reviewed studies demonstrate that intermittent fasting can: reduce body fat percentage without significant muscle loss when protein intake is optimized, improve insulin sensitivity and fasting blood glucose levels, trigger cellular autophagy (cellular repair process) after 16+ hours of fasting, lower LDL cholesterol and blood triglycerides, and reduce markers of systemic inflammation.

Does coffee break a 16:8 fast?
Plain black coffee (no sugar, milk, or cream) does not break a fast. It contains fewer than 5 calories and does not trigger an insulin response. It may even enhance fat oxidation during fasted cardio.
How long until intermittent fasting shows results?
Most people notice reduced bloating and improved energy within 1–2 weeks. Significant body composition changes typically appear after 4–8 weeks of consistent practice paired with proper calorie targets.
Can I work out while fasting?
Yes. Fasted training can increase fat oxidation. For strength training, scheduling your workout close to your eating window opening allows optimal post-workout nutrition. Consume protein within 30–60 minutes of finishing your training session.
Is 16:8 better than 18:6 for weight loss?
Both protocols produce similar results when calories are matched. 18:6 creates a slightly longer autophagy window and may reduce total calorie consumption naturally due to a smaller eating window. Choose the protocol you can sustain long-term.
